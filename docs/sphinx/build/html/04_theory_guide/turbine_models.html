All models represent the rotor as a distribution of Lagrangian force points whose body forces are projected onto the surrounding CFD mesh using different types of projection Gaussian kernel. Optionally, and currently only for the ALM, every turbine in a farm can delegate its structural dynamics and detailed aerodynamics to OpenFAST, while TOSCA handles the background LES flow.

Gaussian Force Projection

The Lagrangian body force at each actuator point is smeared onto the surrounding mesh cells using the regularised delta function

\[g(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{\varepsilon^3 \pi^{3/2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_0|^2}{\varepsilon^2}\right),\]

where \(\mathbf{x}_0\) is the actuator point location and \(\varepsilon\) is the regularisation width. The integration domain is truncated once 99 % of the Gaussian volume has been accounted for. To avoid systematic under-projection and numerical instabilities, \(\varepsilon/\Delta > 2\) is recommended, where \(\Delta\) is the local mesh size.

Notably, other types of projection kernels exist, such as the anisotropic Gaussian, where each direction (blade-aligned, blade-tangential etc) has its own regularization width and the Gauss-exponential kernel, which is used for the AFM due to the coarse background mesh resolution that this model is designed for.

Aerodynamic Force Computation (ADM and ALM)

Both the ADM and the ALM share the same blade-element aerodynamic model, which is summarized in this section.

Blade reference frame. At each actuator point with position \(\mathbf{r}_p\) relative to the rotor centre, a local reference frame \((\hat{\mathbf{x}}_b, \hat{\mathbf{y}}_b, \hat{\mathbf{z}}_b)\) is constructed:

\[\hat{\mathbf{z}}_b = \pm\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}_p, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{x}}_b = -\hat{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{y}}_b = \hat{\mathbf{z}}_b \times \hat{\mathbf{x}}_b,\]

where \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}\) is the unit rotor-axis vector (positive from nacelle to wind), and the sign of \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_b\) follows the rotation convention: for a clockwise rotor \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_b = +\hat{\mathbf{r}}_p\) (root to tip), for counter-clockwise \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_b = -\hat{\mathbf{r}}_p\). This convention keeps the local velocity vector in the first quadrant of the \((x_b, y_b)\) plane in both cases. The CFD velocity \(\mathbf{u}\) is projected onto this frame to give the axial component \(u_{b,x}\) (aligned with wind direction) and tangential component \(u_{b,y}\) (aligned with blade motion).

Angle of attack. The geometric angle of attack at radial station \(r\) is

(1)\[\alpha = \arctan\!\left(\frac{u_{b,x}}{u_{b,y}}\right) - \theta_t - \beta_c - \Delta\beta_\text{wf},\]

where \(\theta_t\) is the local twist angle, \(\beta_c\) is the collective blade pitch, and \(\Delta\beta_\text{wf}\) is an optional pitch control offset that can be applied through an external wind farm control table to simulate coordinated control strategies.

Airfoil table lookup. The lift and drag coefficients are obtained by 2D interpolation in pre-supplied tabular data. Each radial station \(r\) lies between two airfoil tables at radial positions \(r_1\) and \(r_2\), and the coefficients are linearly interpolated in both the radial direction and in angle of attack:

\[C_{l/d}(r, \alpha) = w_1\,C_{l/d}^{(1)}(\alpha) + w_2\,C_{l/d}^{(2)}(\alpha), \qquad w_1 = \frac{r_2 - r}{r_2 - r_1},\; w_2 = 1 - w_1.\]

Prandtl tip/root-loss correction. To account for the finite number of blades, the Prandtl correction factor \(F = F_\text{tip} \cdot F_\text{root}\) is applied, where

(2)\[\begin{split}F_\text{tip} &= \frac{2}{\pi}\arccos\!\exp\!\left(-\frac{N_b}{2}\, \frac{R-r}{r\sin\varphi}\right), \\ F_\text{root} &= \frac{2}{\pi}\arccos\!\exp\!\left(-\frac{N_b}{2}\, \frac{r-r_\text{hub}}{r\sin\varphi}\right),\end{split}\]

with \(N_b\) the number of blades, \(R\) the tip radius, \(r_\text{hub}\) the hub radius, and \(\varphi\) the local flow angle. The corrected coefficients are \(\tilde{C}_{l/d} = F\,C_{l/d}\).

Lift and drag forces. The elemental lift and drag at a blade segment of chord \(c\) and span \(dr\) are

(3)\[L = \tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{u}|^2\,c\,dr\,\tilde{C}_l, \qquad D = \tfrac{1}{2}|\mathbf{u}|^2\,c\,dr\,\tilde{C}_d.\]

The aerodynamic reference frame \((\hat{\mathbf{x}}_a, \hat{\mathbf{y}}_a, \hat{\mathbf{z}}_a)\) places \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_a\) along the local velocity vector, \(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_a\) in the radial direction, and \(\hat{\mathbf{z}}_a = \hat{\mathbf{x}}_a \times \hat{\mathbf{y}}_a\) as the lift direction. The body force imposed on the flow at point \(p\) is then

(4)\[\mathbf{B}_p = -(L\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}_a + D\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}_a).\]

Rotor loads. Thrust and torque are accumulated across all actuator points and weighted by their solidity \(\sigma_p\) to yield the rotor-level loads

(5)\[\begin{split}T &= \sum_p f_{a,p}\,\sigma_p\,\rho, \\ Q &= \sum_p f_{t,p}\,\sigma_p\,\rho\,r_p\cos\psi_\text{cone},\end{split}\]

where \(f_{a,p}\) is the axial force component, \(f_{t,p}\) the tangential component, \(\rho\) the air density, and \(\psi_\text{cone}\) the pre-cone angle. The aerodynamic power is \(P_\text{aero} = Q\,\Omega\), and the electrical power is \(P_\text{gen} = Q_\text{gen}\,\Omega_\text{gen}\,\eta_\text{gen}\).

Actuator Disk Model (ADM)

The ADM represents each rotor as a fixed axisymmetric disk of actuator points distributed in the radial and azimuthal directions. Because the disk is time-steady, the forcing represents the azimuthal average of the blade loading and is suitable for wake studies that do not require blade-passage unsteadiness.

Each point on the ADM disk is assigned a solidity weight that distributes the discrete blade loading over the full revolution. The BEM aerodynamic routine described above is applied at every disk point with the local pitch, chord, and twist from the blade geometry tables.

DIPC helix pitch control. The ADM supports an individual-blade-pitch strategy that creates a helical wake structure. An oscillating pitch offset is superimposed on the collective pitch at each azimuthal position \(\psi\):

(6)\[\Delta\beta_\text{DIPC}(\psi, t) = A\sin\!\left(\psi \pm 2\pi f t\right),\]

where \(A\) is the amplitude (degrees), \(f\) is the helix frequency (Hz), and the sign selects the helix handedness. This option is not available for the ALM.

Actuator Line Model (ALM)

The ALM represents each rotor blade as a radial line of actuator points that physically rotates with the rotor at every time step. This makes it an inherently unsteady three-dimensional model capable of resolving blade-passage effects and tip vortices.

At each time step the blade azimuth \(\psi\) is advanced by \(\Omega\,\Delta t\) (from the drive-train solver described below), and each actuator point is rotated accordingly. The BEM aerodynamic routine is then applied at every point using the instantaneous sampled CFD velocity. The ALM uses the same aerodynamic and control routines as the ADM except for the DIPC helix option, which is not implemented for rotating blades.

Uniform Actuator Disk Model (uniformADM)

The uniformADM does not require detailed blade geometry. The rotor thrust is calculated from a prescribed thrust coefficient \(C_t\), which may be constant or variable (tabulated as a function of a reference velocity). The body force at the \(p\)-th disk element is

(7)\[\mathbf{f}_p = \tfrac{1}{2}\,U_\text{ref}^2\,dA_p\,C_t\,(-\hat{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}),\]

where \(dA_p\) is the rotor area fraction of point \(p\) and \(U_\text{ref}\) is a reference velocity whose computation depends on the chosen sampling strategy (sampleType):

  • rotorUpstream: \(U_\text{ref}\) is sampled on a sampling disk located 2.5 diamters upstream of the rotor centre, aligned with the rotor axis (this should only be used for isolated turbines).

  • givenVelocity: \(U_\text{ref}\) is set directly by the user (this should only be used for isolated turbines in uniform inflow).

  • rotorDisk: the disk-averaged velocity \(\bar{U}_\text{disk}\) is measured on the rotor plane and \(U_\text{ref}\) is recovered via momentum theory, \(U_\text{ref} = \bar{U}_\text{disk}/(1-a)\), where \(a\) satisfies \(C_t = 4a(1-a)\). When \(C_t\) is variable this requires an iterative solve (this is the preferred method for wind farm studies).

Notably, it is the user’s responsibility to ensure consistency between the provided values of \(C_t\) and the sampling strategy. For example, when using the rotorDisk sampling, disk-based thrust coefficients should be provided. Conversely, when using e.g. the rotorUpstream sampling, the provided \(C_t\) values should be based on the upstream velocity.

When a variable-\(C_p\) table is provided, the aerodynamic power is computed as \(P_\text{aero} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,U_\text{ref}^3\,A_\text{rotor}\,C_p(U_\text{ref})\). Otherwise it is derived from thrust and induction: \(P_\text{aero} = T\,(1-a)\,U_\text{ref}\).

Actuator Farm Model (AFM)

The AFM is a single-point coarse-LES model intended for very large wind farms where accurately resolving each rotor’s individual wake is unnecessary. The entire rotor is represented by a single actuator point at the hub location, and the same Ct-based thrust formulation as the uniformADM is applied. Three velocity sampling strategies are available:

  • momentumTheory: the hub-height velocity is used to back-compute \(U_\text{ref}\) from one-dimensional momentum theory. The provided \(C_t\) values should be based on the freestream velocity.

  • rotorDisk: disk-averaged velocity is measured on the rotor plane, and the provided \(C_t\) is interpreted as a disk-based coefficient (preferred stratedy).

  • integral: \(U_\text{ref}\) is computed from the integrated velocity over the projection kernel’s support, and the provided \(C_t\) is interpreted as a disk-based coefficient.

Tower and Nacelle Models

Optional tower and nacelle drag models can be activated independently for any turbine model (includeTwr, includeNacelle). The tower is represented as a series of actuator points along its axis, the nacelle as a single point at the hub. The drag body force at each point follows a standard drag formulation and is projected onto the mesh via the same Gaussian kernel as the blade force.

Rotor Dynamics and Drive Train

For the ADM and ALM (without OpenFAST), TOSCA solves a lumped-parameter drive-train model. The equation of motion for the low-speed shaft is

(8)\[J\,\frac{d\Omega}{dt} = \eta_\text{gbx}\,Q_\text{aero} - i_\text{gbx}\,Q_\text{gen},\]

where \(J\) is the total drive-train inertia referenced to the low-speed shaft, \(\eta_\text{gbx}\) is the gearbox efficiency, \(i_\text{gbx}\) is the gearbox ratio (rotor-to-generator), \(Q_\text{aero}\) is the aerodynamic torque accumulated from the BEM loop, and \(Q_\text{gen}\) is the generator torque. The generator speed is \(\Omega_\text{gen} = \Omega\,i_\text{gbx}\).

A first-order low-pass filter smooths the rotor speed signal used by the generator controller:

(9)\[\begin{split}a &= e^{-\Delta t\,f_\text{filt}}, \\ \Omega_\text{filt}(t) &= (1-a)\,\Omega(t) + a\,\Omega_\text{filt}(t-\Delta t),\end{split}\]

where \(f_\text{filt}\) is the filter cut-off frequency (rad/s).

Control Systems

Generator torque controller. TOSCA implements a classic four-region generator torque controller. Below the cut-in generator speed \(\Omega_\text{ci}\) (region 1) the torque is zero. Between cut-in and the rated region-2 speed \(\Omega_{2}\), the torque rises linearly (region 1½). In region 2 the torque follows the optimal power curve

(10)\[Q_\text{gen} = k_P\,\Omega_\text{gen}^2, \qquad k_P = \frac{\pi\,\rho\,R^5\,C_{p,\text{opt}}}{2\,\lambda_\text{opt}^3\,i_\text{gbx}^3},\]

where \(\lambda_\text{opt}\) is the optimal tip-speed ratio. Between the top of region 2 and rated speed (region 2½) the torque is interpolated linearly, and above rated (region 3) it is held at \(Q_\text{rated}\). A torque rate limiter constrains \(|dQ_\text{gen}/dt| \leq \dot{Q}_\text{max}\) when enabled. Alternatively, the rotor speed can be assigned directly from a lookup table of \(\Omega(U_\text{ref})\) by setting genControllerType = rpmControlCurve and upon providing the appropriate tabulated data.

Blade pitch controller. In region 3 the blade pitch is regulated by a gain-scheduled PID controller. The scheduling gain is

(11)\[G(\beta) = \frac{1}{1 + \beta / \beta_{s2r}},\]

where \(\beta\) is the current pitch angle and \(\beta_{s2r}\) is the pitch angle at which the sensitivity halves. The error signal is \(e = \Omega - \Omega_\text{rated}\), and the commanded pitch is

(12)\[\beta_\text{cmd} = G\!\left(K_P\,e + K_I\int_0^t e\,d\tau + K_D\,\dot{e}\right),\]

saturated to \([\beta_\text{min}, \beta_\text{max}]\). The integral is clamped to prevent wind-up. Setting pitchControllerType = bladePitchCurve replaces the PID with a static \(\beta(U_\text{ref})\) lookup table, which requires the user to provide the appropriate tabulated data.

Nacelle yaw controller. The nacelle yaw controller estimates the mean wind direction from a velocity sample taken 2R upstream of the rotor centre (yawSamplingType = hubUpDistance, currently the only available option in TOSCA). An exponential moving average filters the instantaneous flow angle \(\phi_0 = \text{atan2}(u_y, u_x)\):

(13)\[\phi(t) = \phi(t-\Delta t) + \frac{\Delta t}{\tau_\text{yaw}}\!\left[\phi_0(t) - \phi(t-\Delta t)\right],\]

where \(\tau_\text{yaw}\) is the yaw time constant. When the yaw error \(|\phi - \psi_\text{yaw}|\) exceeds the deadband, the nacelle rotates at the prescribed yaw rate (degrees/s) within the angular bounds yawMin and yawMax. All rotor quantities (rotor axis, rotor direction, rotor centre, up-sampling rig, and actuator points) are rotated accordingly.

Wind farm controller. An external wind farm controller applies time-varying pitch or thrust offsets to all turbines simultaneously. An ASCII time-series file supplies pitch offsets \(\Delta\beta_\text{wf}(t)\) for ADM/ALM turbines, or thrust-coefficient offsets \(\Delta C_t(t)\) for uniformADM/AFM turbines. The values are linearly interpolated in time and added to the individual turbine setpoints at each step.

Upstream Velocity Sampling

All turbine models are accompanied by default with a disk-shaped upsampling grid at 2.5 diameters upstream of the rotor centre. The grid consists of a \(12 \times 12\) azimuth–radial array of points spanning from hub to tip radius. The area weight of each point accounts for the annular area of its radial band, including a redistribution of the hub area over the swept annulus. The sampled velocity components are area-averaged to yield the scalar reference velocity \(U_\text{ref}\), and can be used in uniformADM and AFM if the rotorUpstream sampling strategy is selected. The upsampling grid is also used to output some quantities of interest, such as the thrust coefficient evaluated with the upstream velocity.

Parallel Implementation

To minimise global communication, each turbine is managed by a dedicated sub-communicator TRB_COMM of at most 8 MPI ranks. Only the mesh cells within the turbine’s influence sphere, i.e. the smallest bounding box that contains the rotor at any yaw angle, are mapped to a turbine. Force projection, velocity sampling, and checkpoint I/O are all confined to these rank subsets.

The upsampling disk has its own communicator UPW_COMM. When the nacelle yaws, trbMoved is set and the controlled-cell search is repeated. A single global MPI_Reduce is issued only at output write times to synchronise the yaw angle across the full domain. Each turbine designates a writer rank (rank 0 of TRB_COMM) that handles all checkpoint files and time-series logs.

OpenFAST Coupling

When compiled with -DUSE_OPENFAST, any turbine in the farm can be delegated to OpenFAST for its structural dynamics, detailed aerodynamics (AeroDyn), and generator/pitch control (ServoDyn). In this configuration, TOSCA sends wind information to OpenFAST at every CFD time step, while OpenFAST sends back point positions and loads to TOSCA. Notably, when TOSCA is compiled with OpenFAST support, the user may choose to activate the OpenFAST coupling for only a subset of the turbines in the farm, while the rest run with the native drive-train and aerodynamic models.

Initialisation. TOSCA creates one fast::OpenFAST instance per coupled turbine and populates the coupling metadata structure fi:

  • dtFAST = dtCFD / nFastSubSteps: OpenFAST internal time step;

  • FST input file: turbines/<type>.<id>.fst;

  • restart checkpoint: turbines/<type>.<id>.chkpt.

OpenFAST exposes two independent grids of coupling points per turbine: velocity points (AeroDyn inflow nodes, classified as hub=0, blade=1, or tower=2) and force points (AeroDyn/ElastoDyn structural nodes). All coordinates are maintained in the TOSCA global Cartesian frame. After FAST->init() the number of force and velocity points is confirmed and storage for velPtsBlade, velValsBlade, forcePtsBlade, forceValsBlade, and the tower equivalents is allocated.

Per-step coupling algorithm. The coupling follows a sequential predictor–corrector algorithm with the following steps at every CFD time step:

  1. Point discovery: findControlledPointsRotorOpenFAST assigns each OpenFAST velocity point to its controlling processor in the TRB_COMM communicator. This is required to query the right processor when sampling the actuator point velocity. Ties between processors are broken by a small rank-dependent perturbation so that each point is owned by exactly one rank.

  2. Velocity transfer (TOSCA to OpenFAST): computeWindVectorsRotorOpenFAST and computeWindVectorsTowerOpenFAST trilinearly interpolate the TOSCA velocity field at every OpenFAST velocity point, then call FAST->setVelocity on the writer rank to send the velocity data to OpenFAST.

  3. OpenFAST advance: stepOpenFAST calls FAST->step() nFastSubSteps times per CFD time step, advancing the OpenFAST internal state at the finer resolution dtFAST.

  4. Geometry update: getForcePtsBladeOpenFAST and getForcePtsTwrOpenFAST retrieve the updated structural positions from OpenFAST and broadcast them to all ranks in the TRB_COMM. Then, mapOFDisplToActPts copies these positions to the TOSCA actuator-point arrays.

  5. Force transfer (OpenFAST to TOSCA): mapOFForcesToActPts reads the aerodynamic body forces from the OpenFAST force points and writes them to the TOSCA actuator-point force arrays, scaled by \(1/\rho\). These forces are subsequently projected onto the CFD mesh by the standard Gaussian projector.

Global rotor state. The function getGlobParamsOpenFAST extracts all rotor-level scalars needed by the TOSCA I/O and upsampling infrastructure. Notably, this is done to preserve the workflow used in non-OpenFAST turbines, where these quantities are calculated by TOSCA. For OpenFAST-coupled turbines, TOSCA infers these values from the updated points and the available OpenFAST outputs, so that the same post-processing routines can be used for all turbine models regardless of the coupling status. Obviously, OpenFAST-coupled turbines also have the option to output these quantities directly from OpenFAST if activated in the .fst file. Some examples of the inferred global parameters are:

  • Hub position and shaft direction: obtained via FAST->getHubPos / FAST->getHubShftDir and broadcast to all ranks.

  • Rotor speed \(\Omega\): derived from the angular displacement of the last blade force point (blade-3 tip) between successive steps, \(\Omega = \Delta\theta / \Delta t_\text{CFD}\), where \(\Delta\theta = \arccos(|\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\text{new}\!\cdot\!\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\text{old}|)\).

  • Azimuth: computed from the projection of the tip vector onto the rotor plane; 0 deg when blade 0 points vertically up, increasing in the clockwise direction when viewed from the front.

  • Thrust and torque: obtained from FAST->computeTorqueThrust and projected onto the rotor axis, also independently accumulated from the force-point array for cross-validation and comparison.

  • Rotation direction: inferred from \(\operatorname{sign}\!\left((\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\text{old}\times\hat{\mathbf{r}}_\text{new})\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}\right)\).

Yaw handling. Because OpenFAST (ServoDyn) controls nacelle yaw, TOSCA infers the current yaw angle from the updated shaft direction rather than running its own yaw controller. Whenever the yaw angle changes by more than 1°, the upsampling rig is fully rebuilt: the 12 x 12 azimuth–radial grid is recomputed at 2.5D upstream in the new rotor-normal direction, and trbMoved is set to trigger a new controlled-cell search.
